10 Red Flags in Apartment Leases Most Renters Miss

June 12, 2025
8 min read
By AnalyzeThisContract

Landlords have lawyers. Most renters have a weekend to decide and no one in their corner. Here are the clauses that cost people thousands every year — and how to spot them before you sign.

Most renters spend more time researching a new TV than reading their lease agreement. That's understandable — leases are long, dense, and written by attorneys whose clients are not you. But buried in that language are clauses that can cost you your security deposit, trap you in a renewal, or leave you with no recourse when things go wrong.

We analyzed hundreds of real lease agreements, and the same problematic clauses appear again and again. Here are the ten you should look for before you ever pick up a pen.

1. The Automatic Renewal Clause

Also called an "auto-renew" or "holdover" clause, this provision automatically extends your lease — sometimes for a full year — if you fail to give written notice of non-renewal within a specified window. That window is often 60 days, but some leases require 90.

⚠️ Watch For
Language like: "Tenant shall provide written notice of intent to vacate no fewer than [60] days prior to the lease termination date. Failure to provide such notice shall constitute Tenant's election to renew the lease for an additional [12-month] term."

Set a calendar reminder the day you sign. If your lease ends December 31, your notice deadline may be October 1.

2. Vague or Unlimited Landlord Entry Rights

Most states require landlords to give 24–48 hours notice before entering your unit except in emergencies. But some leases grant far broader rights — entry "at any reasonable time" or "upon reasonable notice" without defining what "reasonable" means.

ℹ️ Know Your Rights
Look up your state's entry notification law. If the lease gives the landlord fewer protections than the law requires, the law usually prevails — but you still don't want to fight about it.

3. Buried Fees That Compound

Late fees are expected. What renters miss are the compounding structures. Some leases charge a flat late fee plus a daily rate after a grace period. A $1,500 rent payment that's 10 days late can accumulate $200+ in fees in leases with aggressive language.

Also watch for: administrative fees, NSF (returned payment) fees, lease renewal processing fees, and move-out inspection fees charged regardless of condition.

4. The "As-Is" Condition Clause

If a lease states you accept the unit "as-is" or "in its current condition," you may have waived your right to demand pre-existing repairs be made. Always document the unit thoroughly — video walkthrough, photos with timestamps — before signing. Get any promised repairs in writing as a signed addendum, not a verbal promise.

5. Renter Responsible for All Repairs Under a Threshold

Some leases make tenants responsible for all repairs under a dollar amount — typically $100–$200. This sounds minor until you're paying a plumber to snake a drain that was clogged on move-in, or replacing a garbage disposal you never used.

⚠️ Red Flag Language
"Tenant shall be responsible for all repairs costing less than $150. Repairs above this threshold shall be the responsibility of Landlord at Landlord's sole discretion."

6. Landlord's Right to Terminate With Short Notice

Most leases protect tenants from arbitrary eviction during the lease term. But some contain clauses allowing the landlord to terminate with as little as 30–60 days notice if they intend to sell, move in themselves, or renovate. These are often legal but rarely disclosed upfront.

Ask directly: "Do you have any plans to sell or occupy this property in the next 12 months?" Get the answer in writing.

7. The Joint and Several Liability Clause

If you're signing a lease with roommates, this clause makes each of you individually responsible for the entire rent — not just your share. If your roommate disappears in month four, you owe the full amount. This is standard language, but most renters don't realize what it means until they're in trouble.

💡 Tip
Consider a roommate agreement separate from the lease that spells out each person's financial obligations and what happens if someone needs to leave early.

8. No Subletting — Ever

Many leases prohibit subletting entirely, or require landlord approval that can be withheld "in the landlord's sole discretion." If there's any chance you'll need to leave before your lease ends — job change, relationship, health — this clause can trap you in a contract with no exit.

Negotiate for: landlord approval not to be "unreasonably withheld," and a buyout clause (typically 1–2 months rent) as an alternative exit.

9. Utilities "To Be Determined"

Leases that leave utility responsibility vague — "utilities as agreed" or "see addendum" without attaching the addendum — create disputes later. Get in writing exactly which utilities are included, which you pay, and how shared utilities in multi-unit buildings are calculated.

10. Waiver of Jury Trial / Mandatory Arbitration

Some residential leases include clauses requiring disputes to go to binding arbitration rather than court, or waiving your right to a jury trial. These clauses significantly limit your options if you need to fight an illegal eviction or wrongful deposit withholding. They're increasingly common and often fully enforceable.

If you see language like "Tenant and Landlord agree to resolve any dispute through binding arbitration..." — understand what you're giving up before you sign.

The Bottom Line

A lease is a legal contract. The fact that it's presented as standard doesn't make every clause non-negotiable. Landlords expect some back-and-forth, especially on terms that fall outside local norms. The more you know about what you're signing, the better your position to ask for changes — or to walk away.
